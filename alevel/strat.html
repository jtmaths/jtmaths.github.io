Stratified Sampling: A Level Maths Revision Guide

Stratified sampling is a key data collection method in A Level Maths. It is a type of random sampling used when your population naturally divides into distinct, non-overlapping groups (called strata), and you want your sample to accurately reflect the proportions of these groups in the overall population.

Common examples of strata include age groups, year groups in a school, or genders.

How It Works

To carry out stratified sampling, you follow these steps:

  1. Divide the population into mutually exclusive strata.
  2. Calculate the proportion of the population that each stratum represents.
  3. Determine the sample size needed from each stratum using a specific formula so that the proportions in the sample match the proportions in the population.
  4. Use simple random sampling to select the individuals from each stratum.

The Formula

In A Level Maths, you will frequently need to calculate how many people/items to sample from a specific stratum. The formula you need is:

Sample size from stratum = (Size of stratum / Total population size) × Total overall sample size

A Practical Example

Imagine a school has 1000 students in total, divided into three year groups. You want to take a stratified sample of 50 students to survey them about school lunches.

Here is the population breakdown:

  • Year 11: 300 students
  • Year 12: 500 students
  • Year 13: 200 students

To find out how many students to survey from each year group, apply the formula:

  • Year 11 Sample: (300 / 1000) × 50 = 15 students
  • Year 12 Sample: (500 / 1000) × 50 = 25 students
  • Year 13 Sample: (200 / 1000) × 50 = 10 students

Check your working: 15 + 25 + 10 = 50, which is your exact target sample size. Once you know these numbers, you would use a random number generator to pick 15 specific Year 11s, 25 Year 12s, and 10 Year 13s from the school register (the sampling frame).

Advantages and Disadvantages

Exam boards love to ask you to evaluate sampling methods. Here is what you need to know for stratified sampling:

Advantages:

  • It perfectly reflects the population structure.
  • It guarantees proportional representation of all groups (unlike simple random sampling, where you might accidentally pick only Year 12 students by pure chance).

Disadvantages:

  • You must have a complete sampling frame (a list of the whole population) that also details which stratum every single individual belongs to.
  • The population must clearly divide into distinct strata, which isn't always naturally possible.
