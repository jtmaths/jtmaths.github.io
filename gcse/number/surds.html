Surds Masterclass

What is a Surd?

A surd is a square root that cannot be simplified into a whole number (integer). It is an "irrational number," meaning its decimal form goes on forever without repeating.

  • \(\sqrt{4} = 2\) (Not a surd, it's a whole number).
  • \(\sqrt{5} = 2.23606...\) (Yes, this is a surd).
  • \(\sqrt{9} = 3\) (Not a surd).

In math exams, we leave answers in "surd form" to be exact, rather than rounding a decimal.

1. The Golden Rules of Surds

There are three main laws you need to memorize to manipulate surds.

Rule 1: Multiplication
$$ \sqrt{a} \times \sqrt{b} = \sqrt{a \times b} $$
Example: \(\sqrt{3} \times \sqrt{5} = \sqrt{15}\)
Rule 2: Division
$$ \frac{\sqrt{a}}{\sqrt{b}} = \sqrt{\frac{a}{b}} $$
Example: \(\frac{\sqrt{20}}{\sqrt{5}} = \sqrt{4} = 2\)
Rule 3: Separation (Simplifying)
$$ \sqrt{a \times b} = \sqrt{a} \times \sqrt{b} $$

This is the reverse of Rule 1. We use this to simplify large surds by finding square factors.

Simplify \(\sqrt{50}\)
Think: "What square number goes into 50?" (4, 9, 16, 25...)
Answer: 25.
$$ \sqrt{50} = \sqrt{25 \times 2} = \sqrt{25} \times \sqrt{2} = 5\sqrt{2} $$

2. Adding and Subtracting

You can only add or subtract surds if they are the same type (like algebra).

Think of \(\sqrt{2}\) like \(x\).

  • \(2\sqrt{2} + 3\sqrt{2} = 5\sqrt{2}\) (Correct ✅)
  • \(\sqrt{2} + \sqrt{3}\) (Cannot be simplified ❌)

3. Expanding Brackets

Treat this exactly like expanding algebraic brackets \((a+b)(c+d)\).

Expand: \((2 + \sqrt{3})(4 - \sqrt{3})\)
1. First: \(2 \times 4 = 8\)
2. Outside: \(2 \times -\sqrt{3} = -2\sqrt{3}\)
3. Inside: \(\sqrt{3} \times 4 = +4\sqrt{3}\)
4. Last: \(\sqrt{3} \times -\sqrt{3} = -\sqrt{9} = -3\)

Collect like terms:
Numbers: \(8 - 3 = 5\)
Surds: \(-2\sqrt{3} + 4\sqrt{3} = +2\sqrt{3}\)
Answer: \(5 + 2\sqrt{3}\)

Question 1: Simplify \(\sqrt{48}\)

We need the largest square number that goes into 48.

Square numbers: 4, 9, 16, 25...

16 divides into 48 exactly 3 times.

$$ \sqrt{48} = \sqrt{16 \times 3} $$ $$ = \sqrt{16} \times \sqrt{3} $$ $$ = 4 \times \sqrt{3} $$ Answer: \(4\sqrt{3}\)
Question 2: Calculate \(\sqrt{75} + \sqrt{12}\)

We cannot add them yet. We must simplify both first so they have a common surd.

Step 1: Simplify \(\sqrt{75}\)
Largest square factor is 25.
\(\sqrt{75} = \sqrt{25 \times 3} = 5\sqrt{3}\)

Step 2: Simplify \(\sqrt{12}\)
Largest square factor is 4.
\(\sqrt{12} = \sqrt{4 \times 3} = 2\sqrt{3}\)

Step 3: Add them
\(5\sqrt{3} + 2\sqrt{3} = 7\sqrt{3}\)

Answer: \(7\sqrt{3}\)
Question 3: Expand and Simplify \((\sqrt{5} + 2)^2\)

Write it out as double brackets:

$$ (\sqrt{5} + 2)(\sqrt{5} + 2) $$

Use FOIL (First, Outside, Inside, Last):

  • First: \(\sqrt{5} \times \sqrt{5} = 5\)
  • Outside: \(\sqrt{5} \times 2 = 2\sqrt{5}\)
  • Inside: \(2 \times \sqrt{5} = 2\sqrt{5}\)
  • Last: \(2 \times 2 = 4\)

Combine: \(5 + 4 + 2\sqrt{5} + 2\sqrt{5}\)

Answer: \(9 + 4\sqrt{5}\)
Question 4: Simplify \(\frac{18}{\sqrt{3}}\) (Rationalizing the Denominator)

We never leave a surd on the bottom of a fraction. To remove it, multiply the top and bottom by \(\sqrt{3}\).

$$ \frac{18 \times \sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{3} \times \sqrt{3}} $$

The bottom becomes \(\sqrt{9}\), which is just 3.

$$ \frac{18\sqrt{3}}{3} $$

Now simplify the fraction \(18 \div 3 = 6\).

Answer: \(6\sqrt{3}\)
Question 5: Solve for \(x\): \(\sqrt{8} \times \sqrt{10} = x\sqrt{5}\)

Step 1: Combine the left side
\(\sqrt{8} \times \sqrt{10} = \sqrt{80}\)

Step 2: Simplify \(\sqrt{80}\)
Find the largest square factor of 80. It is 16.
\(\sqrt{80} = \sqrt{16 \times 5} = 4\sqrt{5}\)

Step 3: Compare
We have \(4\sqrt{5} = x\sqrt{5}\)

Answer: \(x = 4\)
